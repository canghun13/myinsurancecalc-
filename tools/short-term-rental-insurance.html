Why Your Homeowners Policy Doesn't Cover This

Standard homeowners insurance is priced and underwritten for owner-occupied residential use — it explicitly excludes business activity. Renting your property to paying guests, even occasionally, is legally treated as a business use, so a standard homeowners claim can be denied outright if a guest is injured or causes damage during a paid stay. Worse, once an insurer discovers undisclosed short-term rental activity — often during a claim investigation — they can non-renew or cancel your entire policy, not just deny the one claim.

What Airbnb's AirCover Actually Covers (and Doesn't)

Airbnb's AirCover for Hosts markets up to $3 million in damage protection and $1 million in liability coverage at no cost — but it is not an insurance policy. It's a discretionary host protection program, meaning Airbnb decides what qualifies on a claim-by-claim basis, and there's no regulatory guarantee of payout the way there is with a licensed insurance policy. AirCover doesn't cover normal wear and tear, and hosts with six or more active listings may find AirCover applies only as excess coverage — sitting on top of a primary short-term rental policy they're expected to already carry, not replacing it.

What a Real STR Policy Actually Adds

  • Property damage from guest activity — Fire, water damage, vandalism, and accidental damage caused during a paid stay, which a standard homeowners policy may deny once it identifies the activity as commercial.
  • Guest liability coverage — Legal defense and damages if a guest is injured on the property or a guest's own visitor is hurt, typically $500,000 to $1,000,000+ per occurrence.
  • Loss of rental income — Reimbursement for lost booking revenue if the property becomes uninhabitable due to a covered loss, such as a fire or storm.
  • Theft and content protection — Coverage for furnishings and appliances specific to a rental setup, which often exceed what a standard homeowners policy assumes for a single household.

City and County Permit Requirements Are a Separate Issue

Insurance and legal permitting are two different requirements, and cities increasingly require both. Austin, San Antonio, and Houston, for example, require STR registration, and some cities specifically mandate proof of liability insurance as part of that permitting process. Operating without a required local permit can result in fines independent of any insurance question — check your specific city and county rules before you list a property, not after.

How to Keep Costs Down

  • Ask whether your current landlord policy can be endorsed for short-term rental use rather than replaced entirely — some carriers offer an STR endorsement that's cheaper than a fully separate policy.
  • Match your liability limit to your actual exposure — an occasional single-room host has meaningfully less exposure than a full-time whole-home operator, and shouldn't necessarily buy the same limit.
  • Bundle with other policies where possible. Carriers specializing in STR coverage, such as Steadily, Obie, and Proper, sometimes offer multi-property discounts if you operate more than one listing.
  • Screen guests and maintain a clean claims history — like most insurance, a track record of few or no claims meaningfully lowers renewal pricing over time.
  • Compare specialized STR carriers against your current landlord insurer before assuming you need to switch entirely — sometimes an endorsement is enough.
