How Workers Comp Premiums Are Calculated

Workers compensation premiums follow a straightforward formula: (Annual Payroll ÷ 100) × Classification Rate × Experience Modification Factor = Annual Premium. Each component plays a distinct role in your final cost, and understanding all three is essential to managing what is often one of the largest insurance expenses for employers in high-risk industries.

Classification Rate

Every job type is assigned a classification code — most states use NCCI (National Council on Compensation Insurance) codes, though some states like California, Delaware, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, and Texas use their own systems. Each code carries a rate per $100 of payroll that reflects the historical injury costs for that type of work. The spread is enormous: a clerical office worker might be classified at $0.25–$0.40 per $100, a restaurant worker at $1.50–$2.50, a general construction laborer at $6–$10, and a roofer at $15–$25 or higher depending on the state.

Proper classification is critical. Misclassifying employees into lower-rated codes — even unintentionally — creates significant audit exposure. When your insurer conducts the annual premium audit and discovers employees were misclassified, you owe the difference in premium going back to the policy inception date, plus potentially additional penalties. Conversely, if employees are overclassified, you're overpaying. Review classifications with your broker annually.

Many businesses have employees who perform multiple types of work. A construction company owner who spends 80% of their time in the office and 20% on job sites may be able to split their payroll between clerical and construction classifications, significantly reducing premium on the portion legitimately classifiable as clerical. This is legal and appropriate when properly documented.

Experience Modification Factor (X-Mod)

The experience modification factor (X-Mod or EMR) is the most powerful variable in workers comp pricing for established businesses. It compares your actual claims experience to the expected losses for a business of your size in your industry over a three-year period (excluding the most recent year, which hasn't fully developed). A mod of 1.0 means your claims are exactly average for your industry and size. Below 1.0 — say 0.80 — means you pay 20% less than average. Above 1.0 — say 1.25 — means you pay 25% more.

On a $100,000 annual premium, the difference between a 0.75 mod (excellent safety record) and a 1.25 mod (poor safety record) is $50,000 per year. Over five years, that's $250,000 in additional premium — paid every year until your claims history improves. This is why safety investment has such strong financial returns in workers comp: every prevented claim not only avoids the direct cost of the claim but also avoids years of elevated mod surcharges.

The mod is calculated by your state's workers comp rating bureau (NCCI in most states, or the state's own bureau) and cannot be negotiated with your insurer. The only way to improve it is to improve your actual claims experience over time. New businesses without sufficient claims history receive a default mod of 1.0 until they have enough data for experience rating to apply — typically after two to three years. Premium Audit

Workers comp policies are written at the beginning of the year based on estimated payroll. At year end, your insurer conducts a premium audit — reviewing payroll records, tax filings, certificates of insurance from subcontractors, and job descriptions — to determine the actual premium owed. If actual payroll was higher than estimated, you owe additional premium. If lower, you receive a return premium.

Audit surprises are one of the most common and painful workers comp issues for employers. A business that hires seasonal workers, significantly grows payroll mid-year, or misestimates overtime can face an audit bill equal to 25–50% of their original annual premium. Accurate payroll estimation at inception and mid-year payroll reviews with your broker prevent large audit bills.

Subcontractor certificates of insurance are a critical audit issue for general contractors and businesses that use subcontractors. If a subcontractor does not carry their own workers comp and is injured on your job, you may be responsible for their workers comp coverage — and your insurer will add their payroll to your audit. Always collect current certificates of insurance from every subcontractor before work begins.

Workers Comp Rates by Industry: What to Expect

The following approximate rate ranges per $100 of payroll represent national averages — actual rates vary significantly by state and carrier:

  • Clerical / Office: $0.20–$0.45/100 — The lowest-risk classification. Applies only to employees who perform exclusively office work with no field exposure.
  • Retail / Sales: $0.80–$1.50/100 — Slip-and-fall and lifting injuries are the primary claim types.
  • Restaurant / Food Service: $1.50–$2.80/100 — Burns, cuts, slips, and repetitive motion injuries are common. Kitchen staff rates are higher than front-of-house.
  • Light Manufacturing / Assembly: $2.00–$4.00/100 — Machine operation, repetitive motion, and material handling injuries.
  • Heavy Manufacturing / Industrial: $4.00–$8.00/100 — Higher severity injuries including crushing, machinery, and chemical exposure.
  • Warehousing / Distribution: $2.50–$5.00/100 — Forklift accidents and musculoskeletal injuries from lifting are the primary claim drivers.
  • General Construction: $5.00–$10.00/100 — Falls, struck-by incidents, and tool-related injuries. Rate varies by type of work performed.
  • Roofing: $15.00–$25.00/100 — One of the highest classifications due to fall risk. Some states are at the higher end of this range.
  • Electrical / Plumbing: $4.00–$8.00/100 — Electrocution risk, falls, and tool injuries.
  • Trucking / Transportation: $6.00–$12.00/100 — Highway accidents, loading/unloading injuries, and musculoskeletal claims.
  • Oil & Gas / Mining: $8.00–$16.00/100 — Among the highest-risk industries. Rates reflect catastrophic injury potential.
  • Logging / Forestry: $10.00–$20.00/100 — One of the most hazardous occupations in the U.S. by injury rate per worker.

Monopolistic States: No Private Market

North Dakota, Ohio, Washington, and Wyoming require all employers to purchase workers comp directly from the state fund — private insurance is not available in these states. Each state fund operates differently:

  • North Dakota WSI (Workforce Safety & Insurance): All coverage purchased from WSI. Known for proactive safety programs and reasonable rates. Large employers meeting financial requirements may self-insure with WSI approval.
  • Ohio BWC (Bureau of Workers' Compensation): The largest state-operated workers comp program in the nation. Ohio offers group rating programs that allow employers in the same industry to pool risk — participation can result in significant premium reductions for employers with good loss records.
  • Washington L&I (Labor & Industries): Comprehensive program with strong vocational rehabilitation. Washington offers retrospective rating programs (retro) that allow groups of employers to receive premium refunds if their collective claims experience is favorable.
  • Wyoming WC (Department of Workforce Services): Smaller program reflecting Wyoming's population. Self-insurance is available for qualifying large employers.

If you operate in a monopolistic state, your primary lever for cost management is safety performance and, where available, participation in group rating or retro programs. Shopping carriers is not an option.

Texas: The Only Optional State

Texas is the only state in the nation where workers compensation is not mandatory for most private employers. Employers who choose not to carry coverage are called non-subscribers. This sounds appealing from a cost perspective — but non-subscriber employers face a fundamentally different and often more expensive risk: they lose their most important liability defenses.

Standard employer defenses that workers comp immunity provides — the fellow servant rule, contributory negligence, and assumption of risk — are not available to Texas non-subscribers. An injured worker can sue a non-subscriber employer directly for negligence, and there is no cap on damages. A single serious injury lawsuit against a non-subscriber can produce a verdict or settlement far exceeding what years of workers comp premiums would have cost. Most Texas employers — particularly those with more than a handful of employees or any significant physical risk — benefit from carrying workers comp despite it not being required. Texas Mutual Insurance Company is the competitive state fund that provides a market of last resort and competitive pricing, particularly for smaller businesses.

How to Reduce Your Workers Comp Costs

Workers comp costs are more controllable than most employers realize. The following strategies, implemented consistently, can produce meaningful premium reductions over a 3–5 year horizon:

  • Implement a formal written safety program. A documented safety program — with regular training records, incident investigation procedures, and accountability measures — reduces injury frequency. Every claim avoided is a direct premium reduction over the following three years through experience mod improvement. OSHA estimates that for every $1 invested in workplace safety, $4–$6 is returned in reduced injury costs.
  • Build a return-to-work program. Temporary total disability benefits — wage replacement while an injured worker cannot return to work — are typically the largest component of workers comp claim costs. A return-to-work program that provides modified duty for injured workers reduces disability duration dramatically. An injured worker returning to modified duty in two weeks instead of twelve weeks can reduce a claim cost by 60–80%. This single strategy often has the most immediate impact on experience mod.
  • Report injuries immediately. Studies consistently show that claims reported within 24 hours of injury cost significantly less than delayed reports. Early reporting enables timely medical treatment that prevents minor injuries from becoming serious ones, and enables early nurse case management that guides treatment toward appropriate care rather than unnecessary procedures.
  • Review classifications annually. As your business evolves, the work your employees perform may change. An annual classification review with your broker ensures you're not overpaying for employees whose duties have shifted to lower-risk activities. It also ensures compliance — you don't want to be underclassified and face a large audit bill.
  • Manage medical care actively. In states that allow employer-directed medical care (often through a panel of physicians), selecting quality occupational medicine providers who understand work-related injury treatment and return-to-work protocols can significantly reduce both medical costs and disability duration.
  • Consider group rating or self-insurance. Larger employers with good safety records may qualify for self-insurance (retaining the risk directly and paying claims themselves) or group self-insurance pools. Ohio's group rating program is a well-known example. These arrangements can produce significant savings for employers with below-average claims experience.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I shop for workers comp in North Dakota, Ohio, Washington, or Wyoming?

No. These four states are monopolistic — employers must purchase workers comp directly from the state fund (WSI in North Dakota, BWC in Ohio, L&I in Washington, or the Department of Workforce Services in Wyoming). Private insurance isn't available.

Is workers comp mandatory in Texas?

No — Texas is the only state where it's optional for most private employers. But opting out (becoming a "non-subscriber") means losing standard legal defenses like the fellow servant rule and assumption of risk, so an injured worker can sue directly for negligence with no cap on damages.

What's the fastest way to lower workers comp costs?

A return-to-work program that offers injured employees modified duty. Getting a worker back on modified duty in two weeks instead of twelve can reduce a claim's cost by 60–80% — this single strategy often has the most immediate impact on your experience mod.

Does reporting an injury quickly actually save money?

Yes. Studies consistently show claims reported within 24 hours cost significantly less than delayed reports, since early reporting enables timely medical treatment and early nurse case management toward appropriate care.

How much does a formal safety program actually save on premiums?

OSHA estimates that for every $1 invested in workplace safety, $4–$6 is returned in reduced injury costs, and fewer claims also improve your experience modification rate over the following three years.
