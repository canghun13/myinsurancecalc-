Is Renters Insurance Required in Hawaii?

No state, including Hawaii, legally requires tenants to carry renters insurance. That said, individual landlords and property management companies in Hawaii can — and often do — require proof of a policy as a condition of the lease, particularly for larger apartment complexes. Even where it isn't required, renters insurance is one of the most affordable ways to protect your belongings and cover your liability as a tenant.

Average Renters Insurance Cost in Hawaii

Renters in Hawaii pay an estimated $168 per year ($14/month) for a standard policy, based on 2026 industry average data. Actual premiums vary based on coverage limits, deductible, credit history (where allowed), claims history, and the specific building or neighborhood. Renters who only need modest personal property coverage (around $20,000) typically pay noticeably less than this average, while those insuring higher-value belongings pay more.

What a Standard Policy Covers in Hawaii

A typical renters insurance policy in Hawaii covers three main areas: personal property (your belongings, against fire, theft, and other covered perils), liability (if someone is injured in your rental or you accidentally damage someone else's property), and additional living expenses (temporary housing costs if your rental becomes uninhabitable after a covered loss). Standard policies generally exclude flood and earthquake damage, which require separate coverage.

What Makes Hawaii Renters Insurance Unique

Hawaii bans credit-based insurance pricing, and the state's isolated shipping routes push up the replacement cost of electronics and appliances, which factors into personal property premiums.

Risk Factors Specific to Hawaii

Underwriters in Hawaii weigh high replacement costs for electronics and personal property more heavily than in lower-risk states when pricing renters policies. Reviewing your policy's covered perils against these local risks is worth doing before you assume a standard policy covers everything.

How to Save on Renters Insurance in Hawaii

Because Hawaii renters insurance is already relatively affordable compared to homeowners coverage, small changes can meaningfully cut your premium further.

  • Bundle with auto insurance — most insurers offer a 5–15% discount for combining policies.
  • Raise your deductible from $500 to $1,000 to lower your monthly premium.
  • Install security features like deadbolts, smoke detectors, or a monitored alarm system.
  • Compare quotes from at least 3 insurers annually — rates for identical coverage can vary widely.
  • Choose replacement cost coverage over actual cash value; the premium difference is small but the payout difference is significant.

Can insurers use your credit score to set renters insurance rates in Hawaii?

No. Hawaii is one of a handful of states that bans or significantly restricts the use of credit-based insurance scores when pricing renters insurance. Insurers must base your premium on other factors instead, such as coverage limits, claims history, and location.

What's the biggest renters insurance risk factor in Hawaii?

In Hawaii, the leading cost factor is replacement cost rather than any single peril. Shipping and import costs for replacing damaged or stolen belongings run higher here than on the mainland, so it's worth choosing a personal property limit that accounts for these higher effective replacement costs.
