Workers Compensation Requirements in Vermont

Vermont requires workers' compensation for all employers with one or more employees. The Vermont Department of Labor's Workers' Compensation and Safety Division oversees the system. Vermont has a relatively small workers' comp market given its small population.

Workers Comp Rates in Vermont

Workers compensation premiums in Vermont are calculated based on payroll, job classification (NCCI or state classification codes), and the employer's experience modification factor. The average rate of approximately $1.78 per $100 of payroll is a statewide blended average — actual rates vary significantly by job classification. A clerical office worker might pay $0.20-$0.40 per $100, while a roofing contractor might pay $15-$25 per $100.

For example, an employer with $1 million in payroll at the average rate of $1.78 per $100 would pay approximately $17,800 annually before experience modification adjustments. Employers with better-than-average loss experience (experience mod below 1.0) pay less; those with worse experience pay more.

What Makes Vermont Unique

Vermont's small size means limited insurer competition in the workers' comp market. Vermont's economy — heavy in tourism, healthcare, and small manufacturing — generates workers' comp claims that reflect these industry mixes. Vermont has implemented strong safety consultation services through the Department of Labor.

Workers Comp Benefits in Vermont

Vermont provides temporary total disability at 66.67% of average weekly wage. Medical benefits cover necessary treatment. Permanent impairment benefits use a schedule.

Employee Rights in Vermont

Vermont workers may choose their own treating physician after the initial authorized treatment. The Department of Labor provides hearing officers for dispute resolution.

How Workers Comp Premiums Are Calculated in Vermont

Workers compensation premiums in Vermont follow this basic formula: (Payroll / 100) × Classification Rate × Experience Modification Factor = Premium. The experience modification factor (EMR or X-Mod) compares your actual loss experience to the expected loss experience for your industry. An EMR of 1.0 is average; below 1.0 means better-than-average safety record (lower premiums); above 1.0 means worse-than-average (higher premiums).

Job classification codes are assigned based on the type of work employees perform. Accurate classification is critical — misclassifying employees into lower-risk categories can result in premium audits and significant back-premium assessments. An employer with employees in multiple classifications must track payroll by class code throughout the year.

How to Reduce Workers Comp Costs in Vermont

Vermont's small market means fewer insurer options — working with a broker who actively shops the market is important. Vermont's Department of Labor safety consultation services are a free resource for employers seeking to reduce injury rates. Return-to-work programs are valuable in Vermont's tight labor market where replacing injured workers is difficult.

  • Implement a formal workplace safety program — documented safety training reduces injury frequency and improves experience modification over time.
  • Develop a return-to-work program — getting injured workers back on modified duty reduces temporary disability claim duration and cost.
  • Report all injuries promptly — early reporting enables timely medical treatment and claims management, reducing claim severity.
  • Review job classifications annually — ensure employees are classified in the correct (not simply the lowest-cost) classification code.
  • Work with a loss control specialist — many insurers offer free safety consultation services that identify hazard reduction opportunities.
