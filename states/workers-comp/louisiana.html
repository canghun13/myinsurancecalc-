Workers Compensation Requirements in Louisiana

Louisiana requires workers' compensation for all employers with one or more employees. Louisiana has above-average workers' comp costs driven by its oil and gas, maritime, and construction industries. The Louisiana Workforce Commission oversees the system. Louisiana also has specific maritime injury laws (Jones Act, LHWCA) that apply to offshore and waterfront workers.

Workers Comp Rates in Louisiana

Workers compensation premiums in Louisiana are calculated based on payroll, job classification (NCCI or state classification codes), and the employer's experience modification factor. The average rate of approximately $2.08 per $100 of payroll is a statewide blended average — actual rates vary significantly by job classification. A clerical office worker might pay $0.20-$0.40 per $100, while a roofing contractor might pay $15-$25 per $100.

For example, an employer with $1 million in payroll at the average rate of $2.08 per $100 would pay approximately $20,800 annually before experience modification adjustments. Employers with better-than-average loss experience (experience mod below 1.0) pay less; those with worse experience pay more.

What Makes Louisiana Unique

Louisiana's offshore oil and gas industry creates a complex workers' comp and maritime law environment. Offshore workers may be covered by the Longshore and Harbor Workers' Compensation Act (LHWCA), the Jones Act, or state workers' comp — depending on their specific work location and duties. This distinction is critical and requires specialized legal and insurance expertise.

Workers Comp Benefits in Louisiana

Louisiana provides temporary total disability at 66.67% of average weekly wage. Medical benefits cover necessary treatment. Louisiana's oil and gas workers may be covered by federal maritime law rather than state workers' comp.

Employee Rights in Louisiana

Louisiana workers select their own treating physician from a list of approved physicians. Disputed claims are heard by workers' compensation judges within the Workforce Commission.

How Workers Comp Premiums Are Calculated in Louisiana

Workers compensation premiums in Louisiana follow this basic formula: (Payroll / 100) × Classification Rate × Experience Modification Factor = Premium. The experience modification factor (EMR or X-Mod) compares your actual loss experience to the expected loss experience for your industry. An EMR of 1.0 is average; below 1.0 means better-than-average safety record (lower premiums); above 1.0 means worse-than-average (higher premiums).

Job classification codes are assigned based on the type of work employees perform. Accurate classification is critical — misclassifying employees into lower-risk categories can result in premium audits and significant back-premium assessments. An employer with employees in multiple classifications must track payroll by class code throughout the year.

How to Reduce Workers Comp Costs in Louisiana

Louisiana offshore employers must understand the distinction between state workers' comp and federal maritime law (Jones Act/LHWCA) — the applicable law significantly affects coverage costs and benefits. Oil and gas employers should work with specialized marine and energy insurance brokers. Strong safety programs in high-hazard industries produce significant premium savings.

  • Implement a formal workplace safety program — documented safety training reduces injury frequency and improves experience modification over time.
  • Develop a return-to-work program — getting injured workers back on modified duty reduces temporary disability claim duration and cost.
  • Report all injuries promptly — early reporting enables timely medical treatment and claims management, reducing claim severity.
  • Review job classifications annually — ensure employees are classified in the correct (not simply the lowest-cost) classification code.
  • Since Louisiana workers' comp is written by competing carriers, shop your policy at each renewal — rates for identical classification codes and payroll can vary meaningfully between insurers.
