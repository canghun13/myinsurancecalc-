Minimum Coverage Requirements

Arizona requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of 25/50/15 — meaning $25,000 per person for bodily injury, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $15,000 for property damage. PIP coverage is not required in Arizona, though it may be available as an optional add-on. These are the legal minimums; most insurance professionals recommend carrying significantly higher limits to protect personal assets in a serious accident. A common recommendation is at least 100/300/100, which provides substantially more protection without a proportionally large premium increase.

Average Car Insurance Costs in Arizona

Drivers in Arizona typically pay approximately $1,520 per year ($127/month) for car insurance, based on 2026 industry average data. Individual premiums vary significantly based on driving record, age, vehicle type, annual mileage, and selected coverage levels. Drivers with clean records may see rates well below the state average, while those with violations, claims, or high-risk profiles may pay substantially more.

State Regulations and Key Rules

Arizona is a tort (at-fault) state. Uninsured motorist coverage is not required but must be offered by insurers. The state has relatively low property damage minimums, which may be insufficient for newer vehicles.

What Makes Arizona Unique

Arizona's hot climate and hail storms in the monsoon season make comprehensive coverage a wise addition. The state also has a higher-than-average rate of auto theft in urban areas.

Uninsured Drivers in Arizona

Approximately 12.0% of drivers in Arizona are estimated to be uninsured. Carrying uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage provides important protection against the financial impact of an accident with an uninsured or underinsured driver. Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage pays for your medical expenses and vehicle damage if you are hit by a driver without insurance or with insufficient coverage. Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage fills the gap when the at-fault driver's policy limits are too low to cover your losses.

Risk Factors Specific to Arizona

Arizona's monsoon season (July–September) brings sudden flash flooding, hail, and dust storms (haboobs) that can cause significant vehicle damage. Comprehensive coverage is particularly important during these months.

How to Save on Car Insurance in Arizona

Phoenix and surrounding metro areas have elevated theft rates — anti-theft devices and GPS tracking systems can reduce comprehensive premiums. Arizona's strong insurer competition means shopping quotes annually pays off. Telematics programs (pay-per-mile or drive-monitoring) suit the many Arizona residents who drive less in extreme summer heat.

  • Compare quotes from at least 3–5 insurers annually — rates for identical coverage can vary by hundreds of dollars.
  • Arizona's minimum liability requirement of 25/50/15 is the legal floor, not a safe target — since PIP isn't required in Arizona, a separate medical payments (MedPay) add-on is worth pricing out. Most agents recommend at least 100/300/100 for meaningful asset protection.
  • Ask about bundling discounts if you also need home or renters insurance.
  • With an estimated 12.0% of Arizona drivers uninsured, carrying uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage above the state minimum is one of the highest-value add-ons you can buy.
  • Review your coverage annually, especially after major life changes: moving, buying a new vehicle, or adding a teen driver.
