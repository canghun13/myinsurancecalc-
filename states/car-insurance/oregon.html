Minimum Coverage Requirements

Oregon requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of 25/50/20 — meaning $25,000 per person for bodily injury, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $20,000 for property damage. Personal Injury Protection (PIP) is also required, covering medical expenses regardless of fault. These are the legal minimums; most insurance professionals recommend carrying significantly higher limits to protect personal assets in a serious accident. A common recommendation is at least 100/300/100, which provides substantially more protection without a proportionally large premium increase.

Average Car Insurance Costs in Oregon

Drivers in Oregon typically pay approximately $1,380 per year ($115/month) for car insurance, based on 2026 industry average data. Individual premiums vary significantly based on driving record, age, vehicle type, annual mileage, and selected coverage levels. Drivers with clean records may see rates well below the state average, while those with violations, claims, or high-risk profiles may pay substantially more.

State Regulations and Key Rules

Oregon is a no-fault state requiring PIP of $15,000. Uninsured motorist coverage is also required. Oregon uses a modified comparative fault rule (51% bar).

What Makes Oregon Unique

Oregon's PIP requirement provides $15,000 in medical coverage regardless of fault. The state's rainy climate increases accident frequency, particularly during autumn and winter months.

Uninsured Drivers in Oregon

Approximately 10.9% of drivers in Oregon are estimated to be uninsured. Carrying uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage provides important protection against the financial impact of an accident with an uninsured or underinsured driver. Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage pays for your medical expenses and vehicle damage if you are hit by a driver without insurance or with insufficient coverage. Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage fills the gap when the at-fault driver's policy limits are too low to cover your losses.

Risk Factors Specific to Oregon

Portland and the Willamette Valley see heavy rainfall from fall through spring, increasing hydroplaning and accident risk. Mt. Hood and Cascade passes present serious winter driving hazards. Eastern Oregon sees ice and snow on high-desert highways.

How to Save on Car Insurance in Oregon

Portland rates are above the state average due to congestion, theft, and higher repair costs. Oregon's rainy season (October–April) increases accident risk — adjusting driving habits and maintaining adequate collision coverage is advisable. Bundling home and auto is effective with major carriers in the state.

  • Compare quotes from at least 3–5 insurers annually — rates for identical coverage can vary by hundreds of dollars.
  • Oregon's minimum liability requirement of 25/50/20 is the legal floor, not a safe target — since Oregon already requires PIP coverage, review whether that limit alone would cover a serious injury claim. Most agents recommend at least 100/300/100 for meaningful asset protection.
  • Ask about bundling discounts if you also need home or renters insurance.
  • With an estimated 10.9% of Oregon drivers uninsured, carrying uninsured/underinsured motorist coverage above the state minimum is one of the highest-value add-ons you can buy.
  • Review your coverage annually, especially after major life changes: moving, buying a new vehicle, or adding a teen driver.
