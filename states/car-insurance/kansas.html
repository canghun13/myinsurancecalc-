Minimum Coverage Requirements

Kansas requires drivers to carry minimum liability insurance of 25/50/25 — meaning $25,000 per person for bodily injury, $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $25,000 for property damage. Personal Injury Protection (PIP) is also required, covering medical expenses regardless of fault. These are the legal minimums; most insurance professionals recommend carrying significantly higher limits to protect personal assets in a serious accident. A common recommendation is at least 100/300/100, which provides substantially more protection without a proportionally large premium increase.

Average Car Insurance Costs in Kansas

Drivers in Kansas typically pay approximately $1,280 per year ($107/month) for car insurance, based on 2026 industry average data. Individual premiums vary significantly based on driving record, age, vehicle type, annual mileage, and selected coverage levels. Drivers with clean records may see rates well below the state average, while those with violations, claims, or high-risk profiles may pay substantially more.

State Regulations and Key Rules

Kansas is a no-fault state requiring PIP coverage of at least $4,500 for medical expenses. Uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage is also required. The state sees frequent severe weather including hail and tornadoes.

What Makes Kansas Unique

Kansas requires both PIP and uninsured motorist coverage, giving drivers strong baseline protection. Comprehensive coverage is especially important due to the state's high risk of severe weather damage.

Uninsured Drivers in Kansas

Approximately 10.0% of drivers in Kansas are estimated to be uninsured. Carrying uninsured and underinsured motorist coverage provides important protection against the financial impact of an accident with an uninsured or underinsured driver. Uninsured motorist (UM) coverage pays for your medical expenses and vehicle damage if you are hit by a driver without insurance or with insufficient coverage. Underinsured motorist (UIM) coverage fills the gap when the at-fault driver's policy limits are too low to cover your losses.

Risk Factors Specific to Kansas

Kansas sits in Tornado Alley — comprehensive coverage is essential, not optional. Hailstorms cause significant vehicle damage annually, particularly in central and eastern Kansas. The state also sees frequent ice storms in winter.

How to Save on Car Insurance in Kansas

Kansas City area rates are higher than the state average due to urban congestion and theft. Wichita drivers benefit from the city's competitive insurance market. Raising your comprehensive deductible can lower premiums while still protecting against the significant hail and tornado risk in the region.

  • Compare quotes from at least 3–5 insurers annually — rates for identical coverage can vary by hundreds of dollars.
  • State minimums are typically insufficient for serious accidents — consider liability limits of at least 100/300/100.
  • Ask about bundling discounts if you also need home or renters insurance.
  • Consider telematics or usage-based insurance if you drive fewer miles than average or primarily drive during off-peak hours.
  • Review your coverage annually, especially after major life changes: moving, buying a new vehicle, or adding a teen driver.
