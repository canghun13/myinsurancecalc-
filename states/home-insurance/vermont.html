Home Insurance in Vermont: What You Need to Know

Vermont homeowners face significant risk from winter weather and flooding — Tropical Storm Irene (2011) caused catastrophic flooding that washed out roads and damaged thousands of homes across the state. The 2023 Vermont floods caused over $500 million in damage, striking communities that had barely recovered from Irene. Standard policies cover wind and snow damage but not flooding.

Average Homeowners Insurance Costs in Vermont

Homeowners in Vermont typically pay approximately $1,020 per year ($85/month) for homeowners insurance in 2026, based on industry average data for a home with approximately $290,000 in dwelling coverage. Actual premiums vary significantly based on the home's age, construction type, location, claims history, selected coverage limits, and deductible. Homes in high-risk areas — particularly those facing wildfire, hurricane, or flood exposure — may pay significantly more than the state average.

Natural Disaster Risk in Vermont

Primary risks: Flooding (high), Winter storms (very high), Nor'easter (high), Wildfire (low)

Vermont's river valleys face severe flash flooding risk — narrow valleys can experience rapid water rise from upstream rainfall. Montpelier has flooded catastrophically twice in recent memory. Nor'easters and winter storms deposit heavy snow that can cause roof failure in older structures. Ice dams are common in winter.

What Makes Vermont Unique

Vermont's 2023 floods — which struck in July — were a stark reminder that inland flooding risk is severe even in a small, landlocked state far from the Gulf Coast. Many Vermont homeowners who had gone without flood insurance since Irene found themselves facing the same uninsured losses a second time. Vermont's mountain topography channels water into narrow valleys where flooding can be extremely rapid.

How to Save on Home Insurance in Vermont

Vermont homeowners in river valleys and low-lying areas should obtain flood insurance — the state has experienced two major flood disasters in 12 years. Montpelier, which flooded severely in both 2011 and 2023, is a vivid example of the risk. Adequate snow load coverage is important for older Vermont homes with heavy timber framing.

  • Compare quotes from at least 3-5 insurers — premiums for identical coverage can vary by hundreds or thousands of dollars annually.
  • Bundle your home and auto insurance with the same carrier for discounts typically ranging from 10-20%.
  • Install a monitored home security system — most insurers offer discounts of 5-15% for qualifying systems.
  • Review your dwelling coverage limit annually to ensure it reflects current construction costs, not just your home's market value.
  • Consider whether you need flood insurance separately — standard homeowners policies do not cover flood damage regardless of cause.

What Standard Homeowners Insurance Covers (and What It Doesn't)

A standard HO-3 homeowners policy in Vermont typically covers your dwelling structure and attached structures against most perils (fire, wind, hail, lightning, vandalism, and theft), your personal property against named perils, liability protection if someone is injured on your property, and additional living expenses if your home is uninhabitable due to a covered loss.

Standard policies in Vermont do not cover flood damage (requires separate NFIP or private flood policy), earthquake damage (requires separate earthquake endorsement or policy), normal wear and tear, or intentional damage. Given Vermont's specific risk profile, homeowners should carefully evaluate whether additional coverage types are warranted.
