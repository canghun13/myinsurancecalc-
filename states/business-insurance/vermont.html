When Is Workers' Compensation Required in Vermont?

Vermont's workers' compensation requirement: 1 employee. Sole proprietors and business owners without employees are generally exempt from carrying it for themselves, though LLC members and corporate officers may be included or excluded depending on their management role and how many are involved. If you're unsure whether a specific worker triggers the requirement — especially part-time, seasonal, or 1099 contractor arrangements — it's worth confirming with Vermont's workers' compensation agency directly, since misclassifying an employee as a contractor can result in retroactive premiums and penalties.

Workers' Comp Insurance Market in Vermont

Vermont allows businesses to purchase workers' compensation coverage from private insurance carriers, so it's worth comparing quotes from multiple companies rather than accepting the first offer. Pricing depends on your industry classification code, total payroll, and claims history (reflected in your experience modification rate).

Estimated Small Business Insurance Cost in Vermont

A standard Business Owner's Policy (BOP) — which bundles general liability and commercial property coverage — costs an estimated $650 per year for a small business in Vermont, based on 2026 industry average data. Actual premiums vary significantly based on industry classification, payroll size, claims history, and specific coverage limits. Businesses with higher liability exposure (contractors, food service, retail with public foot traffic) typically pay more than low-risk professional services operating primarily online or by appointment.

General Liability Insurance: Not Required, But Often Necessary

Unlike workers' comp, general liability insurance isn't legally mandated in Vermont for most businesses. That said, it's frequently required contractually — commercial landlords typically require proof of general liability before signing a lease, many client contracts (especially government and enterprise clients) require it, and certain licensed professions in Vermont require it as a condition of maintaining their license. Even businesses with no legal obligation to carry it are exposed to third-party injury and property damage claims from day one of operation.

What Makes Vermont Business Insurance Unique

Vermont requires workers' comp from the first employee, and its low population density and low commercial litigation rate keep small business insurance among the more affordable in New England.

LLC Owners and Workers' Comp in Vermont

If your business is structured as an LLC in Vermont, workers' comp treatment depends on whether members hold management authority. LLC managers who can sign contracts or make business decisions on the company's behalf are typically excluded from the requirement, while non-management members working for the LLC generally must be covered. This exemption is usually capped at a limited number of managers regardless of total membership, so larger LLCs should confirm their specific obligation rather than assuming full exemption.

How to Lower Your Business Insurance Costs in Vermont

  • Bundle general liability and property coverage into a BOP — it's typically cheaper than buying each policy separately.
  • Classify workers correctly from the start — reclassification penalties and back premiums are far more expensive than getting it right the first time.
  • Shop your workers' comp classification code — a misclassified job code can meaningfully overstate your premium.
  • Maintain a documented safety program — many insurers offer credits for formal workplace safety and loss-prevention practices.
  • Since Vermont's workers' comp market is open to private insurers, shop that policy annually alongside your BOP — rates for identical coverage can vary significantly between carriers.
