Non-Owner SR-22 Insurance: Cost, Coverage & How It Works

7 min read · Updated July 2026

If you need to file an SR-22 but don't own a car — maybe you rely on a family member's vehicle, rideshares, or public transit — a non-owner SR-22 policy is almost always the answer. It satisfies the filing requirement without insuring a specific vehicle, and it's typically cheaper than a full owner's policy. But it comes with real limitations that trip people up, and the certificate itself isn't always the same document depending on your state. Here's exactly what it covers, what it costs, and how to avoid the most common (and expensive) mistake people make with it.

What a Non-Owner SR-22 Policy Actually Covers

Non-owner SR-22 insurance provides liability-only coverage: bodily injury and property damage you cause to someone else while driving a car you don't own. There's no collision or comprehensive coverage, because there's no specific vehicle attached to the policy for the insurer to underwrite. Coverage also applies secondary to the vehicle owner's own policy — if you borrow a friend's car and cause an accident, their insurance pays first, and your non-owner policy only kicks in if the damages exceed their limits.

What it does not cover is just as important:

  • Damage to the car you're driving — that falls to the vehicle owner's collision coverage, or you're personally on the hook if they don't have it.
  • Your own injuries, unless your state's personal injury protection (PIP) rules extend coverage to you as a driver.
  • Any vehicle registered in your name. This is the big one — see the section below.

How Much Non-Owner SR-22 Insurance Costs

Non-owner SR-22 insurance costs an estimated $631 a year on average nationally, but state-level rates vary dramatically — from around $262 a year in lower-cost states like South Dakota up to $1,400+ a year in higher-cost states like New Jersey. If your SR-22 stems from a DUI specifically, expect to pay somewhat more than the general average, often closer to $650 a year, since insurers still price in the underlying violation on top of the non-owner discount. On top of the premium, insurers typically charge a one-time or per-term SR-22 filing fee of $15 to $25.

Even with the DUI surcharge factored in, non-owner SR-22 coverage is still meaningfully cheaper than a comparable owner's policy with an SR-22 attached — you're not paying for collision or comprehensive coverage on a vehicle, and non-owner drivers statistically log fewer miles than someone insuring their daily-driver car.

SR-22 vs. SR-22A vs. FR-44: Not the Same Certificate Everywhere

Most states use a standard SR-22, but a handful of states require a stricter or different version depending on the violation:

Certificate Where It Applies Key Difference
SR-22Most statesStandard certificate; state-minimum liability limits
SR-22AGeorgia, Missouri, Texas (certain offenses)Full premium due upfront — no installment plan; a lapse ends the policy outright
FR-44Florida, Virginia (DUI-related)Requires substantially higher liability limits than a standard SR-22

SR-22A and FR-44 requirements are set by state law and the nature of the violation, not by your insurer. Confirm which one applies to you with your state's DMV or your insurance provider before shopping for coverage.

The Mistake That Trips People Up: Buying a Car While on Non-Owner Coverage

A non-owner policy explicitly excludes any car registered in your name. If you buy or lease a vehicle while covered under a non-owner SR-22, you need to switch to an owner policy before you drive it — continuing to drive under your old non-owner policy means you're effectively uninsured for that car, and a claim would likely be denied. Worse, driving uninsured while an SR-22 requirement is active can extend your filing period or trigger a new suspension entirely.

The fix is straightforward: contact your insurer as soon as you're about to take ownership of a vehicle, before you drive it. Most insurers can convert your non-owner SR-22 to an owner policy and re-file the certificate within 24 hours. As long as there's no gap between the old filing being canceled and the new one taking effect, your continuous SR-22 period isn't interrupted — cancel the old policy too early, though, and a reported lapse can reset your entire filing clock.

Who Actually Needs Non-Owner SR-22 Coverage

  • Your license was suspended and you're required to file an SR-22, but you don't currently own a car.
  • You regularly borrow a family member's or friend's vehicle and want your own liability protection as a secondary layer.
  • You moved to a state that requires an SR-22 based on an out-of-state violation, but you don't own a vehicle locally yet.
  • You want to maintain continuous insurance history — and satisfy your SR-22 requirement — while you're between vehicles.

If none of these apply and you own a vehicle you regularly drive, you need a standard owner's policy with an SR-22 attached instead — a non-owner policy won't satisfy the requirement for a car registered in your name.

How to Keep Costs Down

  • Compare quotes from multiple insurers. Non-owner SR-22 pricing varies significantly by carrier, and not every insurer even offers non-owner policies — regional carriers sometimes beat national ones in specific states.
  • Confirm the filing fee is included in your quote. Some insurers quote the premium separately from the $15–$25 SR-22 filing fee; make sure you're comparing full costs.
  • Never let coverage lapse. A lapse is reported to your state and typically resets your entire filing period — the single most expensive mistake to avoid.
  • Ask about completing a defensive driving course. Some insurers offer a modest discount for drivers who complete an approved course, even with an SR-22 on file.
  • Re-shop once your SR-22 period ends. Rates typically drop noticeably once the filing requirement is lifted (usually after 3 years) — don't assume your current insurer will automatically lower your rate without you asking or shopping around.

Bottom Line

Non-owner SR-22 insurance does exactly one job well: it satisfies your state's filing requirement and provides liability protection when you drive a borrowed or rented car, without the expense of insuring a vehicle you don't own. The coverage is narrow by design, and the single costliest mistake is continuing to drive a newly purchased car under a non-owner policy — switch to owner coverage the moment you take ownership, and time the transition so there's no reported gap in your filing.
